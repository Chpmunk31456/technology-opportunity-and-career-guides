# Choose Your Technology Career

**Version 1.0**  
**Author:** Alberto (Al) Leiva

> **Career guidance and safety disclaimer:** This material supports education and career exploration. It is not psychological testing, medical advice, legal advice, rehabilitation evaluation, or licensed counseling. Personality and interest tools describe preferences, not intelligence, ability, or destiny. No job, degree, salary, or family expectation is more important than a person's safety and life. If career pressure is contributing to hopelessness or thoughts of self-harm, stop career planning and seek immediate help from a trusted person, qualified professional, local emergency service, or crisis service.


## Start with the whole person

A good career choice is not based on personality alone. It combines interests, current strengths, skills you are willing to learn, values, accessibility needs, family responsibilities, available time, finances, location, and the realities of the labor market.

## Use personality tools carefully

Myers-Briggs-style personality frameworks may help someone describe preferred ways of communicating, deciding, organizing, or restoring energy. They do not measure intelligence, competence, mental health, or career potential. No one should be directed toward or excluded from a career because of a four-letter type.

A stronger approach combines several sources:

1. **Interests:** Use the free [O*NET Interest Profiler](https://www.mynextmove.org/explore/ip).
2. **Skills:** List what you can demonstrate today and what you are willing to practice.
3. **Values:** Identify what matters most, such as stability, social impact, income, remote work, creativity, or public service.
4. **Work preferences:** Consider independent work, teamwork, structure, uncertainty, public contact, and physical environment.
5. **Practical circumstances:** Include internet access, equipment, time, caregiving, transportation, work authorization, language, and disability accommodations.
6. **Career experiments:** Test a pathway with a short course, small project, informational interview, or volunteer contribution.

## Matching patterns - possibilities, not rules

- People who enjoy investigation may explore cybersecurity, data science, forensics, AI evaluation, or research.
- People who enjoy building may explore software, cloud, platform engineering, robotics, or IoT.
- People who enjoy helping and explaining may explore accessibility, assistive technology, consulting, product management, training, or technical support.
- People who prefer structure and evidence may explore GRC, privacy, data governance, quality assurance, or FinOps.
- People who enjoy creative ambiguity may explore AI product work, UX research, conversational design, innovation, or architecture.

Every personality can succeed in every field. Fit depends on the specific workplace, manager, team, duties, and accommodations - not only the title.

## A safe three-career experiment

For each of three possible careers:

1. Read five real job descriptions from official employer pages.
2. Complete one free introductory lesson.
3. Try one two-to-four-hour project.
4. Speak with one person in or near the field.
5. Record what felt interesting, difficult, meaningful, or inaccessible.
6. Decide whether to continue, modify, or stop. Stopping an experiment is useful information, not failure.

## Basic career-support skills for mentors

Mentors and community volunteers can learn active listening, open-ended questions, strengths identification, summarizing, goal setting, cultural humility, disability awareness, privacy, ethical boundaries, and referral skills. These practices do not make someone a licensed counselor or vocational evaluator.

When a person expresses hopelessness, says they are a burden, or talks about self-harm, career planning must stop. Listen calmly, take the statement seriously, and help connect the person with immediate qualified support. Do not promise secrecy when there is imminent danger.

## AI use disclaimer

AI can help organize information, compare career paths, improve grammar, and practice interviews. It can also produce outdated, incomplete, or biased recommendations. Verify important information through official sources and do not share unnecessary medical, financial, immigration, identity, or confidential employment information.
